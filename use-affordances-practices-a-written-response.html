Use, Affordances, & Practices: A Written Response
Responses to the lecture and the readings
Bill Gaver, Technology Affordances
Bret Victor, A Brief Rant on the Future of Interaction Design
From this week’s lecture, Professor Ansari discussed the topics of ‘affordances’ and ‘practices’ from various designers and philosophers. From Heidegger’s perspective, I found it very interesting when he compared human’s mind with a computer as a central processor that analyzes data people take in. Connecting to the ‘affordances’, people learn from actions, history, and things around us. While affordances define the possibilities of interactions, every experience requires a learning process. Heidegger did not touch a lot on false affordances, but the idea of “the more intuitive it is, the more 'ready-to-hand' the interaction” also implied that good design should have good signifiers.
Similarly, the reading “Technology Affordances” suggested that “making affordances perceptible is one approach to designing easily-used systems.” However, I think it’s still difficult to make affordances clear to everyone, considering people’s differences in age, culture, and capability to perform tasks. Younger kids might not know that a Save button icon is a shape of hard disk because they’ve never seen one, while older people might have troubles understanding minimal design.
Forest Young’s conversation with Sara Hendren also touched on service design and designing for people with disabilities. It reminds me of the Microsoft Inclusive Design Toolkit, “solve for one, extend to many.” When inclusive design is considered, it works across a spectrum of related abilities, like the curb cuts example. Apart from user experience design, the conversation made me think about the role of communication design in affordances and signifiers. Since the goal is to make affordances perceptible, what should designers consider when introducing new affordances to the users? For example, when mobile developers came up with swiping, how did they make different groups of users adopt it? Or thinking back to the design process, how did they know that people will adopt that new interaction?
